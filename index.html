Fault Lines: Blame Attribution in Online Discourse During the 2026 Lebanon Conflict

01

Introduction

On 28 February 2026, a joint United States–Israeli operation targeting Iran's Supreme Leader, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, triggered a rapid escalation across the Israel–Lebanon conflict. Subsequent exchanges between Israel and Hezbollah led to major civilian displacement and casualties within Lebanon.

This escalation occurred in the context of a fragile post-2024 ceasefire and an incomplete process of Hezbollah disarmament within Lebanon. Ongoing Israeli strikes during the ceasefire period and the partial implementation of disarmament measures contributed to a highly unstable equilibrium prior to March 2026.

This report analyses how responsibility for the escalation was attributed in online discourse, with particular attention to variation across geographic contexts. It examines how blame attribution patterns correspond to political and sectarian alignments and proximity to the conflict.

Data & Methodology

This study draws on a dataset of 48,407 tweets collected via the X (Twitter) API using Lebanon conflict-related keywords in Arabic and English, covering the period 12–19 March 2026. The dataset spans 150 countries and two languages (69.4% English, 30.6% Arabic).

Tweets were classified along two dimensions: (1) blame attribution, identifying which actors were held responsible (Israel, Iran, Hezbollah, the Lebanese government, the sectarian system, or the international community), and (2) discursive stance (analytical, anti-war, pro-resistance, pro-Israel or unclear). Classification was conducted using the Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct language model. Blame attribution categories were non-mutually exclusive, allowing individual tweets to assign responsibility to multiple actors.

Geographic location was inferred from user profile data. A country of origin was identifiable for 44.9% of tweets. For analytical purposes, tweets were grouped into three categories: accounts based in Lebanon (n = 4,013), accounts in Arab-majority countries (n = 7,123), and accounts in Western countries (n = 7,334). Within these groups, activity is unevenly distributed: Saudi Arabia accounts for 42% of tweets in the Arab-majority group, while the United States (33%) and United Kingdom (22%) dominate the Western group.

Several limitations should be noted. Firstly, X/Twitter users are not representative of the general population and tend to skew younger and more urban. Secondly, 55% of tweets lack identifiable geographic data which may introduce selection bias. Lastly, “country of account” reflects self-reported profile location rather than nationality or identity. This is particularly relevant in countries with large Arab diaspora populations (e.g. 65% of tweets from Germany are in Arabic), where geographic classification may not align with the political or cultural context of the speaker.

Accordingly, the findings should be interpreted as an analysis of online discourse within a specific platform and time period rather than as a measure of public opinion.

02

Displacement and Escalation

The escalation in early March 2026 resulted in large-scale internal displacement across Lebanon. On 5 March, evacuation orders were issued for all areas south of the Litani River, affecting more than 100 villages. On 6 March, these orders were extended to Beirut's southern suburbs, leading to the rapid displacement of approximately 700,000 residents within a 24-hour period.

By 12 March, over 816,000 people had been displaced, according to the Ministry of Social Development. Displacement was geographically concentrated in predominantly Shia areas, with affected populations relocating to Sunni, Christian, and Druze-majority regions, placing significant pressure on shelter capacity nationwide.

Casualty figures also increased rapidly during this period. By 20 March, over 1,000 fatalities had been reported, alongside more than 128 recorded attacks on medical facilities (Health Ministry).

Between 12 and 19 March, the dataset records 48,407 tweets in Arabic and English from accounts across 150 countries. During the early phase (12–17 March), discourse was relatively balanced between Arabic (54%) and English (46%). By 18–19 March, English-language tweets accounted for 82% of the sample.

This shift suggests a transition from primarily regional discourse to increased international attention as the escalation intensified. The change in language distribution is consistent with a broader expansion of the audience engaging with the conflict online.

The following sections examine how this expansion in attention is reflected in patterns of blame attribution: which actors are held responsible, how this varies across geographic contexts and time.

Variation Within Geographic Groups

The aggregate geographic categories reveal important country-level differences.

In the Arab-majority group, Saudi Arabia alone accounts for 42% of tweets, and the Gulf states collectively comprise 72% of the sample. The high level of Iran-directed blame in this category therefore reflects a concentration within Gulf discourse rather than a uniform Arab pattern. Egypt and Qatar depart from this trend, with both attributing more blame to Israel than to Iran.

Within the Western group, the United States shows the highest pro-Israel stance (16%) of any non-Israel country, while Ireland records the highest anti-war share (41%). Outside the three primary groupings, India and Pakistan are distinguished by a predominantly analytical stance (61–63%).

Interpreting Iran-Directed Blame

Iran-directed blame is concentrated primarily in Arab-majority contexts, particularly in Gulf states, and is expressed almost entirely in Arabic. The dataset contains no Farsi-language tweets and only 13 tweets from accounts based in Iran, indicating that this pattern is not driven by Iranian domestic discourse.

Within Europe, a similar pattern emerges in countries with large Arab diaspora populations (e.g. Germany, Sweden, France, Denmark, and Belgium). In these contexts, Iran-directed blame is disproportionately expressed in Arabic (e.g. 78% of such tweets in Germany, 94% in Denmark, and 68% in Sweden).

Taken together, these patterns suggest that what appears as “Western” attribution of blame to Iran partly reflects transnational Arabic-language discourse rather than geographically bounded public opinion. In this sense, language functions as a more reliable indicator of discursive alignment than country-level classification alone.

Stance Analysis

In addition to blame attribution, tweets were classified by discursive stance. Accounts based in Lebanon show the highest proportion of analytical content (54%) and the lowest levels of explicitly partisan positioning among all groups.

By contrast, pro-resistance sentiment is more prevalent in several Arab-majority contexts, including Tunisia (48%), Yemen (27%), and Iraq (24%). Notably, this stance is also more common among Arab diaspora accounts (17%) than among Lebanon-based accounts (10%).

This distribution suggests that proximity to the conflict may be associated with more analytical and less polarised forms of discourse, while more distant or diaspora contexts exhibit higher levels of explicitly partisan framing. This relationship is descriptive and does not imply causation, but it is consistent across the dataset.

Temporal Shifts During Escalation

The dataset spans eight days and is divided into an early period (12–17 March) and a spike period (18–19 March), during which the intensity of military activity and international media attention increased. The shift in discourse between these periods is measurable.

Blame attributed to the sectarian system falls from 19.9% to 7.1% (–12.8 percentage points), representing the largest shift in the dataset. Attribution to the Lebanese government (–5.2pp) and Iran (–5.8pp) also declines. By contrast, attribution to proximate conflict actors increases, with Israel rising from 37.4% to 42.2% (+4.8pp) and Hezbollah from 24% to 30.3% (+6.3pp).

From Structural to Event-Driven Framing

During the early period, blame attribution—particularly among Lebanon-based accounts—was relatively distributed across multiple actors, including the Lebanese government (28%), the sectarian system (24%), and Israel (27%). This pattern reflects a more structural interpretation of the crisis, emphasising governance failures and longer-term political arrangements.

During the spike period, this distribution narrows. References to systemic and institutional actors decline, while attribution increasingly concentrates on those directly engaged in military activity. This shift is consistent with a move from structural to event-driven framing, in which immediate developments take precedence over broader explanatory narratives.

05

Discussion

Key Findings

The analysis indicates that geographic location is closely associated with patterns of blame attribution in online discourse. Accounts based in Lebanon distribute blame across a wider range of actors and exhibit the highest proportion of analytical content. In contrast, accounts in Gulf states show a higher concentration of Iran-directed blame, consistent with prevailing regional political narratives. Accounts in Anglophone Western countries are more likely to attribute responsibility to Israel, reflecting a focus on observable state military action in English-language discourse.

The temporal analysis further suggests that escalation is associated with a narrowing of discourse. As the intensity of violence increases, attention shifts away from systemic and institutional factors toward immediate actors directly involved in the conflict. This is reflected in the decline of references to the sectarian system and domestic governance during the spike period, alongside increased attribution to Israel and Hezbollah.

Beyond shifts in blame attribution, the displacement itself also emerged as a distinct focus of online discourse.

Displacement and Inter-Communal Tension

A supplementary analysis of 12,338 displacement-related tweets suggests that the large-scale movement of populations from southern Lebanon and Beirut's southern suburbs was framed less in material than in political and sectarian terms. Sectarian framing was the most common category (53%), followed by securitisation (39%) and exclusionary rhetoric (31%), while references to economic or service burden were comparatively rare (3.6%). This indicates that displacement was discussed primarily as a question of identity, security, and communal balance rather than humanitarian management or state capacity.

Geographic variation within this subset is also notable. Lebanon-based accounts show the lowest level of exclusionary rhetoric (20%) and the highest level of solidarity framing (27%), whereas accounts in Arab-majority countries—particularly in the Gulf—display substantially higher rates of exclusionary language (43%). This pattern is consistent with the broader finding that proximity to conflict is associated with less polarised and less exclusionary forms of discourse.

Within Lebanon, language also appears to shape framing. English-language tweets show higher levels of sectarian framing than Arabic-language tweets, while Arabic posts are more likely to adopt practical or informational tones. This may reflect differences in audience orientation, with English-language discourse more focused on external explanation and political framing.

Interpretation

Taken together, these findings suggest that online discourse during conflict is shaped by a combination of geographic context, linguistic community, and proximity to events. In particular, proximity to the conflict appears to be associated with more distributed and analytical forms of attribution, while more distant or diasporic contexts exhibit more concentrated and partisan framings.

The observed shift from structural to event-driven attribution during escalation highlights a potential constraint for policy communication. Narratives that emphasise institutional reform or long-term structural factors may become less salient during periods of acute violence, when attention is focused on immediate actions and responsibility.

Limitations

These findings are descriptive and do not establish causal relationships between geographic location and patterns of discourse. The dataset reflects activity on a single platform over a limited time period and is subject to selection bias, including incomplete geographic data and the demographic skew of X/Twitter users.

Accordingly, the analysis should be interpreted as an account of observable discourse within a specific digital environment, rather than as a measure of broader public opinion.
