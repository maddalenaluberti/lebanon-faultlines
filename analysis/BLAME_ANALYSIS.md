# Blame Attribution in the 2026 Lebanon Conflict: Cross-Tabulation Analysis

**Dataset**: 49,149 tweets (48,407 after excluding classification errors)
**Period**: March 12-19, 2026
**Languages**: English (33,617), Arabic (14,790)
**Research question**: Who gets scapegoated in the 2026 Lebanon conflict, and does it differ between people inside Lebanon, the diaspora, and international observers?

---

## Methodology

### Location grouping

Tweets were classified into five geographic groups based on the `_country` field:

| Group | Countries | n |
|-------|-----------|---|
| **Lebanon** | Lebanon | 4,013 |
| **Diaspora (Arab)** | Saudi Arabia, Iraq, UAE, Kuwait, Jordan, Syria, Egypt, Bahrain, Qatar, Oman, Yemen, Libya, Tunisia, Algeria, Morocco, Sudan, Palestine, + regional labels (West Asia, North Africa) | 7,123 |
| **Western** | US, UK, Canada, Australia, NZ, France, Germany, Netherlands, Sweden, Ireland, Italy, Spain, Belgium, Austria, Switzerland, Denmark, Norway, Finland, Portugal, Greece, Poland, Cyprus, + regional labels (Europe, North America, Australasia, Eastern Europe) | 7,334 |
| **Other Intl** | India, Israel, Pakistan, Turkey, Nigeria, South Africa, etc. | 3,266 |
| **Unknown** | Empty country field | 26,671 |

### Time periods

- **Early** (Mar 12-17): 16,680 tweets -- initial escalation
- **Spike** (Mar 18-19): 31,727 tweets -- major escalation, nearly 2x volume

### Note on blame targets

Tweets can have multiple blame targets. Percentages represent "% of tweets in this group that mention this target" and will sum to more than 100%.

## 1. Blame x Location

### Cross-tabulation: % of tweets mentioning each blame target

| Blame target | Lebanon (n=4013) | Diaspora Arab (n=7123) | Western (n=7334) | Other Intl (n=3266) | Unknown (n=26671) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Israel** | 32.5% | 27.2% | **48.8%** | **50.7%** | 41.8% |
| **Iran** | 21.4% | **55.4%** | 29.4% | 30.9% | 27.4% |
| **Hezbollah** | 19.7% | 25.4% | 27.5% | 30.9% | 29.9% |
| **Lebanese govt** | **23.1%** | 15.6% | 14.8% | 9.4% | 11.5% |
| **Sectarian system** | **17.6%** | **25.2%** | 11.5% | 5.8% | 7.7% |
| **Intl community** | 4.6% | 3.7% | 6.6% | 4.6% | 5.4% |
| **No clear blame** | **24.1%** | 11.6% | 15.3% | 16.0% | 22.2% |

### Key findings

**Finding 1: The Arab diaspora overwhelmingly blames Iran -- Lebanese people do not.**
The single most striking finding in this dataset. 55.4% of Arab diaspora tweets blame Iran, compared to just 21.4% of Lebanese tweets -- a **34 percentage point gap**. This is the largest divergence in the entire analysis. The Arab diaspora (dominated by Saudi, Iraqi, and Gulf voices) reads this conflict through an Iran-axis lens. Lebanese people, living inside the crisis, distribute blame more evenly.

**Finding 2: Lebanese tweeters uniquely blame their own government and the sectarian system.**
Lebanon is the only group where the Lebanese government (23.1%) and sectarian system (17.6%) feature prominently as blame targets. No outside group blames domestic Lebanese governance at comparable rates. This suggests an internal political critique that external observers largely miss -- for Lebanese people, the conflict is not just geopolitical but a failure of their own political system.

**Finding 3: Western and international observers focus overwhelmingly on Israel.**
48.8% (Western) and 50.7% (Other Intl) blame Israel, far exceeding the 32.5% rate among Lebanese themselves. The Western lens simplifies the conflict into an Israel narrative, while Lebanese people see a more complex web of responsibility.

**Finding 4: Lebanese tweets are the most likely to assign no clear blame (24.1%).**
This could reflect the paralysis and complexity of living inside the crisis -- when you can see all actors failing simultaneously, singular blame becomes harder to assign.

**Finding 7: Pro-Israel sentiment is overwhelmingly English-language.**
12.1% of English tweets are pro-Israel vs. just 3.6% of Arabic tweets. This 3.4x ratio reflects both the geography of English-language audiences and the near-total absence of pro-Israel framing in Arabic discourse.

## 3. Blame x Time

### Overall temporal shift (Early vs. Spike)

|---|---|---|---|
| Israel | 37.4% | **42.2%** | +4.8pp |
| Iran | **35.3%** | 29.5% | -5.8pp |
| Hezbollah | 24.0% | **30.3%** | +6.3pp |
| Lebanese govt | **16.8%** | 11.6% | -5.2pp |
| **Sectarian system** | **19.9%** | **7.1%** | **-12.8pp** |
| Intl community | 4.4% | 5.6% | +1.2pp |
| No clear blame | 17.0% | 20.5% | +3.5pp |

### The scapegoat shifted: from systemic critique to wartime actors

**Finding 8: The single largest temporal shift was the collapse of "sectarian system" as a blame target.**
From 19.9% in the early period to just 7.1% during the spike -- a 12.8 percentage point drop. When the crisis was still developing, there was space for structural critique. Once full-scale escalation hit on Mar 18-19, blame consolidated around wartime actors (Israel +4.8pp, Hezbollah +6.3pp) while systemic critique evaporated.

**Finding 9: Iran blame also declined during escalation (-5.8pp).**
This is counterintuitive -- as the conflict escalated, blame shifted away from the proxy-war framing (Iran) and toward the direct combatants (Israel, Hezbollah). The discourse became more "who is killing people right now" and less "who is pulling the strings."

### Key findings

**Finding 13: Lebanese tweeters are the most analytical and least partisan.**
54.0% of Lebanese tweets are classified as analytical -- nearly double the rate of diaspora or Western audiences. Only 13.1% take a clear pro-resistance or pro-Israel position combined. Living in the crisis produces more analysis, less cheerleading.

**Finding 14: Pro-resistance sentiment peaks in the Arab diaspora, not inside Lebanon.**
17.0% of Arab diaspora tweets vs. 10.0% of Lebanese tweets are pro-resistance. This is the "armchair resistance" pattern -- those furthest from the bombs are most likely to champion armed resistance.

**Finding 15: Pro-Israel sentiment is a Western/international phenomenon.**
Only 3.1% of Lebanese tweets are pro-Israel. In Western countries it is 11.8% -- nearly 4x higher. Pro-Israel framing is virtually absent among people actually living in Lebanon.

## Summary: Compelling Findings for Academic Application

### The headline: Three audiences, three scapegoats

The 2026 Lebanon conflict is not one discourse but three, defined by geography and language:

1. **Inside Lebanon**: Blame is distributed and structural. Lebanese tweeters uniquely blame their own government (23.1%) and the sectarian system (17.6%). They are the most analytical (54%), the least partisan (only 13.1% take a clear pro- position), and the most likely to assign no clear blame (24.1%). The conflict is understood as a systemic failure, not just an external attack.

2. **The Arab diaspora**: Iran is the primary scapegoat (55.4%), with the conflict read through a Sunni-Shia proxy war lens. The "Iran + sectarian system" pairing (8.9% of tweets) is a distinctive frame that treats sectarianism itself as an Iranian export. Pro-resistance sentiment (17.0%) is higher than inside Lebanon -- the "armchair resistance" effect.

3. **Western observers**: Israel dominates (48.8%), with one in four Western tweets blaming Israel alone. Pro-Israel sentiment (11.8%) is nearly 4x the Lebanese rate. The "both sides" frame (Hezbollah + Israel) is most common here. Structural critique is nearly absent.

### The temporal story: Escalation kills nuance

The most dramatic finding may be the temporal shift. In the early period (Mar 12-17), all groups engaged in more structural critique -- blaming the sectarian system, the Lebanese government, questioning root causes. Once the escalation spiked (Mar 18-19), discourse collapsed into wartime blame: Israel and Hezbollah surged, structural critique evaporated (-12.8pp for sectarian system blame overall). For Lebanese voices specifically, the shift from "our system is broken" (sectarian system at 24.2% early) to "we are under attack" (Israel at 37.5% spike) was the most dramatic transformation of any group.

### The language divide: Two parallel conflicts

Arabic and English Twitter discuss fundamentally different conflicts. English Twitter's top blame target is Israel (46.5%). Arabic Twitter's is Iran (45.2%). These nearly mirror-image numbers tell the story of a discourse split along linguistic -- and therefore cultural, geographic, and political -- lines. Structural critique (sectarian system, Lebanese government) is almost exclusively Arabic-language. The English-language conversation about Lebanon is simpler, more actor-focused, and more polarized between pro-Israel and pro-resistance positions.

### The surprise: Saudi pro-resistance

Saudi Arabia is the single largest source of pro-resistance tweets outside Lebanon (417 tweets, vs. Lebanon's 401). This grassroots-vs-government split -- where Saudi popular opinion champions Lebanese resistance while Saudi state policy opposes the Iranian axis -- is a finding that complicates simplistic geopolitical narratives.
