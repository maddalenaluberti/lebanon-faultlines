# Potential Research Angles — Lebanon Tweet Dataset

Based on what's actually in the data (50K tweets, March 12-19, 2026).

---

## Angle 1: "Going Global" — How Regional Conflict Becomes International Discourse

**The pitch**: The dataset captures the exact moment the Lebanon war conversation broke out of regional/Arabic-language Twitter into global/English-language mainstream. On Mar 12-17 it's 54% Arabic, mostly regional voices. By Mar 18-19 it flips to 82% English, driven by viral journalist footage and military escalation. How does the framing change when a conflict goes global?

**What you'd analyze**:
- Compare framing terms before/after the shift (resistance vs terrorism, civilians vs militants)
- Which events triggered international attention (journalist attack, civilian casualties, diplomatic statements)
- How diaspora voices differ from in-Lebanon voices vs international observers
- Whether Arabic and English tweets frame the same events differently

**Data strength**: Very strong. 100% coverage on language, timestamps, framing keywords. The temporal spike gives a natural before/after comparison. Quote tweets (17%) show reframing in action.

**UCL appeal**: Timely, original dataset, clear methodology. Fits media studies, conflict communication, or IR policy framing.

---

## Angle 2: Framing the Same War — Arabic vs English Discourse Comparison

**The pitch**: Two parallel conversations happening about the same conflict. Arabic tweets center regional context (Iraq, Syria, Yemen, sectarian dynamics). English tweets focus on the Israel-Iran-Hezbollah nexus. Same events, different frames. What do these different framings reveal about how the conflict is understood across linguistic communities?

**What you'd analyze**:
- Side-by-side hashtag analysis (Arabic has wider regional frame, English is narrower)
- Framing vocabulary: Arabic uses "resistance" (مقاومة) 2.9%, "occupation" (احتلال) 3.5% vs English "terrorism" 5.2%, "genocide" 2.0%
- Source credibility: which news outlets get shared in each language
- Who gets @mentioned — different authority figures in each language

**Data strength**: Very strong. Clean 70/30 EN/AR split, rich text for both, hashtags and mentions fully available.

**UCL appeal**: Comparative discourse analysis with bilingual data is rare. Sara's Arabic expertise is a natural asset here.

## Angle 3: Diaspora vs Homeland — How Location Shapes Conflict Narratives

**The pitch**: 4,048 tweets from in-Lebanon vs ~19,500 from diaspora/international (among the ~23K tweets with country data). Do people in Lebanon talk about the war differently than the diaspora? Different concerns, different framing, different engagement patterns?

**What you'd analyze**:
- Lebanon tweets are 85% Arabic; diaspora is mostly English — language itself is a framing choice
- Engagement patterns: do in-Lebanon tweets get amplified? ignored?
- Topic differences: ground-level reporting vs geopolitical commentary
- Emotional tone: proximity to conflict vs observer distance
- Sub-segments: Arab world diaspora vs Western diaspora vs in-Lebanon

**Data strength**: Moderate. The 53.5% missing-country gap is a limitation — need to acknowledge it. But 23K tweets with clean country data is still substantial. Could supplement with bio-based location detection.

**UCL appeal**: Diaspora studies angle is strong for a policy masters. Direct policy relevance (whose voices get heard in conflict narratives?).

---

## Angle 4: The Viral Moment — How a Single Event Reshapes Conflict Discourse

**The pitch**: The Steve Sweeney journalist attack (Mar 19) generated 1,170 direct mentions and drove the absolute peak hour to 2,074 tweets. Zoom in on how a single viral incident reshapes the entire conversation — new actors, new framing, new audience. A micro-study of discourse contagion.

**What you'd analyze**:
- Pre vs post Sweeney: who's talking, what language, what frame
- How the footage spread: quote tweet chains, key amplifiers, cross-language transmission
- Whether the incident shifted the dominant frame (from military operations to press freedom/war crimes)
- Comparison with other spike events (Beirut building, Hezbollah missiles) that didn't go as viral

**Data strength**: Strong. Conversation threads, quote tweet chains, timestamps, engagement metrics all available. Natural experiment design.

**UCL appeal**: Focused, manageable scope. Clear methodology. Relevant to media studies and conflict journalism policy.

---

## What WON'T Work

- **Youth vs older demographic split**: No age data exists. Account creation date is too weak a proxy.
- **Sentiment analysis as primary method**: Possible but generic. The framing/discourse angles above are more original.
- **Comprehensive network analysis**: Would need follower/following data we don't have (just mentions and replies).

---

## Recommended Combination

Angles 1 and 2 naturally combine: the temporal shift (regional → global) AND the linguistic framing comparison (Arabic vs English) tell a coherent story. Angle 3 (diaspora) could be a supporting section within that. This gives Maddy a rich, multi-layered analysis without needing data the dataset doesn't have.
